## Technical Deep Dive: My Session Notes


I've expanded my notes from the sessions into three major pillars that are currently defining the "curious" edge of the industry:

![image-2](/images/blogs/pyconf-hyd-2026/image-2.jpg)

### 1. Making AI Agents Consistent & Observable
Building agents is easy; making them reliable is the hard part. The session on the **Microsoft Agent Framework** was a game-changer for me.
* **The Consistency Problem:** We discussed why agents often "hallucinate" their way out of a workflow. The solution? **Linear Workflows** tracked via **Open-Telemetry**.
* **Checkpointing:** We explored saving states at intermediate points. This is essential because if an agent fails at step 9 of 10, you shouldn't have to restart from step 1.
* **Verification Layers:** Moving beyond simple prompts to adding layers of authentication before an agent can "act" on the real world.

### 2. The Architecture of Self-Healing Systems (Resilience)
The talks by engineers from **Straive** and **ASBL** on system health were incredibly grounded. They treated recovery as a **State Machine**, not a lucky guess.
* **Hysteresis & Signal Processing:** We learned not to react to the first sign of trouble. By using **Hysteresis**, we wait for a stable pattern of failure before triggering a recovery, which prevents the dreaded **Thundering Herd problem** (where recovery efforts actually crash the system).
* **The Circuit Breaker Pattern:** Implementing `pybreaker` logic—Sense, Decide, Act, Verify, Stop. 
* **Idempotency:** A key takeaway—your recovery logic *must* be idempotent. It should leave the system in the same state regardless of whether the fix was applied once or ten times.
* **Staggered Fixes:** Using **Tenacity** to add jitter and staggering to our fixes, ensuring the system "stretches" back to health rather than snapping.

### 3. The Strategic Use of SLMs (Small Language Models)
The industry is moving away from "Maximum Size" to "Maximum Efficiency." We analyzed benchmarks like **MMLU** and **GPQA** to determine when a small model outperforms a giant.
* **The SLM Toolkit:** We looked at purpose-built models like **Nuextract 2.0** (for data extraction), **Paddle OCR**, and **Qwen 3.5**.
* **The Distillation Strategy:** Why you should consider quantizing a large model or distilling its "knowledge" into a smaller, faster student model.
* **The "Don't Use" List:** A crucial reality check—don't use SLMs for multi-hop reasoning, context windows over 32k, or complex code generation.
